## Habitat & Ecology

The Segmented Bucket-Hat Wader occupies shallow, still-water margins—flooded reed beds, oxbow lagoons, and the slack-current fringes of lowland river systems—where its five-segment column can remain stably upright with three to four segments submerged. It ranges across warm-temperate to subtropical wetlands, favoring water depths precisely matched to its body height; individuals are rarely found where the waterline would breach the neck junction below the hat, suggesting strong depth-selection behavior rather than passive drift.

Lacking limbs, *S. caputbalneus* relies entirely on internal buoyancy regulation within its segments to float and rotate in place. This sessile-adjacent lifestyle places it as a passive suspension feeder in the food web: submerged lower segments are lined with ciliated pores (unfigured but inferred from feeding traces) that draw in plankton and detritus suspended in slow currents, making the species a minor but steady converter of particulate organic matter into biomass available to predatory wading birds and small aquatic carnivores.

The species' most consequential ecological trait is its oversized black brim. Functioning as a fixed parasol, the hat casts a stable disc of shade over the water immediately surrounding the creature's single eye and upper segment—shade that attracts phototactic-averse invertebrates seeking refuge from surface glare. This concentration effect turns the Wader into an inadvertent lure, drawing prey species directly into range of its filtering segments below. Field observations consistently document the Wader in fixed-distance association with its known companion species (the red-bodied, green-hatted counterpart), the two apparently sharing shaded microhabitat patches without competitive displacement, hinting at a mutualistic or at minimum commensal pairing whose mechanism remains under study.
