## Cultural Context: The Panfolk of the Dry Reaches

Among the scattered pastoral communities bordering the salt flats, the *Rectangulus biscuitus* is known colloquially as the **Panfolk** or **Hearth-Slab** — a name derived not from its faceless, immobile form, but from the reverence with which it is treated in domestic ritual.

### Origin Myth
Local oral tradition holds that the Panfolk were the "first bread," baked by an unnamed earth-mother before she learned to make bread that could be eaten. Dissatisfied with her failed loaves, she breathed life into them instead, and they crept away to lie flat across the world, waiting. The six dots down each creature's middle are said to be the mother's fingerprints, pressed in while testing the dough — a detail herders point to as proof that the creature is not manufactured, but *born wrong*.

### Practical Use
Despite (or because of) its immobility, the Panfolk is prized as a **living hearthstone**. Villagers place the creature atop cooking fires, where its thick outer rind resists scorching and its wobbly inner seam is said to "breathe" heat evenly across a pan's surface. Some households keep a single specimen for generations, passed down as kitchen inheritance.

### Symbolism
The six dots are widely interpreted as a ledger of fortune — three per column representing hearth, harvest, and household. A Panfolk whose dots appear symmetrical is considered auspicious; asymmetry, however slight, is read as an omen requiring a small offering of grain laid across its slab. Because it has no face, no mouth, and no eyes, it is regarded as an impartial witness — invoked in oaths sworn "before the flat one," who cannot lie, cannot see, and therefore cannot be deceived.
