## Habitat & Ecology

*Trilobula spinifera* inhabits the leaf-litter horizon and shallow duff of temperate cloud-forest floors, favoring perpetually damp microhabitats beneath fallen logs, moss mats, and root tangles where humidity remains near saturation. Its range is patchy and localized, tracking narrow elevational bands where fog condensation keeps the substrate consistently moist; populations rarely persist in seasonally drying leaf litter.

Lacking legs entirely, the creature relies on peristaltic contraction of its three fused lobes to inch forward, using its bristled snout tip as a probing anchor driven into loose substrate to haul the body mass along—a slow, deliberate locomotion suited to a sedentary detritivore lifestyle. Within the litter food web, *T. spinifera* occupies a decomposer-grazer niche, rasping fungal hyphae, bacterial films, and softened plant debris from the underside of decaying wood using mouthparts housed at the bristle cluster. In turn, its soft, unarmored blob-body makes it a favored prey item for small litter-dwelling predators such as centipedes and shrews, positioning it as a mid-trophic link that transfers microbial biomass upward into the broader detritivore community.

The single eye, seated oddly on the narrow neck rather than the head tip, appears to serve a narrow ecological function tied to the animal's most distinctive feature: the seven-bristle burst at the snout's end. Because the eye cannot resolve detail at the probing tip itself, *T. spinifera* appears to rely on the bristles as the primary sensory array—detecting vibration, air movement, and chemical cues from potential fungal food patches or approaching predators—while the neck-eye serves only to confirm gross light-dark orientation, helping the animal stay oriented toward shaded, sheltered microhabitat as it blindly probes ahead with its whiskered nose.
