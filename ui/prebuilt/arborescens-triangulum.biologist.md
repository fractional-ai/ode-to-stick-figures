## Anatomy

*Proboscipes crinicapita* exhibits one of the more radical departures from conventional tetrapod body architecture documented in the field: a complete fusion of head and body into a single tapering mass, with no discernible torso, thoracic cavity distinction, or limb girdles. The organism stands roughly 0.6 m in height, broadest at the crown and narrowing to a hardened, dark point at the ventral terminus — a structure believed to function as a counterbalancing keel during locomotion.

Sensory architecture is compressed into the upper-left quadrant of the face, where two small dark ocular dots sit in diagonal offset, granting a narrow stereoscopic field oriented perpetually downward and to the left. Below these lies a single elongated dark marking resembling a nostril or lacrimal duct, though dissection records (regrettably scarce, given the species' evasiveness) suggest it is vestigial and non-functional — possibly a relic of an ancestral single-nostril respiratory pore now superseded by cutaneous gas exchange.

Locomotion is achieved not through legs but through a pair of long, ridged, tapering tendrils extending from the chin region — functionally analogous to elephant trunks, structurally closer to muscular hydrostats such as octopus arms. The animal advances by alternately curling and extending these appendages, dragging its body forward in a slow, deliberate inching gait.

## Diet & Life Cycle

*P. crinicapita* is believed to be a subsurface detritivore, using its tendrils to probe leaf litter and soil for fungal mats and root exudate, which it absorbs through specialized epidermal pores near the tendril tips rather than ingesting through a true mouth. Reproduction is presumed asexual, via budding from the ground-mound base on which the animal perpetually rests — the mound itself may represent a semi-permanent rhizoid anchor rather than mere substrate.

## Notable Adaptation

Most striking is the animal's profoundly asymmetric mane: a shaggy, bristled fringe restricted entirely to the top and right side of the head. This lopsided pelage is hypothesized to serve as unilateral thermal and UV shielding, allowing the creature to orient a single flank toward sun exposure while keeping the opposite, balder side pressed toward shaded microhabitat — an elegant solution for a creature with no means of rotating a distinct body, only its axis of lean.
