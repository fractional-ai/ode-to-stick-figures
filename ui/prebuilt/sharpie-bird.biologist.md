## *Rostrolonga alabrachium*

### Anatomy

The Long-Beaked Wing-Arm Bird presents one of the more puzzling skeletal reorganizations in the avian lineage: the cervical vertebrae have been almost entirely resorbed during embryonic development, fusing the cranial dome directly to the trunk. This yields the species' characteristic neckless silhouette, with the head permanently tilted forward under its own mass. The elongated, flattened beak functions as a substrate-scraping and filtering tool, its small chin flap likely serving a tactile or sensory role when foraging in low-visibility conditions such as murky shallows or leaf litter.

Most striking is the animal's profound bilateral asymmetry: a single, well-developed wing—edged with jagged, zigzag notching reminiscent of overlapping flight feathers—occupies the right side of the body, while the left side terminates in a slender, near-vegetative limb resembling a stiff stick, marked only by a small perpendicular tick near its tip. This limb is structurally vestigial, lacking musculature sufficient for flight, but retains a single hardened barb that may aid in balance or territorial displays.

### Diet

*R. alabrachium* is an opportunistic omnivore, using its flattened beak in a shoveling motion to sift small invertebrates, seeds, and organic debris from soft substrates.

### Life Cycle

Juveniles hatch with both limbs equally reduced; the wing does not begin its asymmetric elongation until the second molt, suggesting a delayed, hormonally-triggered developmental cascade unique to this genus.

### Notable Adaptation

The single functional wing, rather than being a deformity, allows the bird to execute a rotational "sculling" flap—generating short bursts of lift while the stick-limb acts as a counterbalancing rudder, a solution to flight that trades efficiency for stability in gusty, ground-level habitats.
