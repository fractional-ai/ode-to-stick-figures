## Habitat & Ecology

*Caniselachus bidens* inhabits the brackish deltas and tidal mudflats of the temperate coastal fringe, favoring the interzone where mangrove root systems give way to open estuarine sandbars. It ranges along low-energy coastlines from river mouths to the upper reaches of tidal marsh, rarely venturing more than two kilometers inland or into water deeper than its own body length. This amphibious tolerance places it at the boundary of two food webs, aquatic and terrestrial, a niche its anatomy is uniquely suited to exploit.

As a mesopredator, the Shark-Dog forages during low tide, using its stubby clawed legs to traverse exposed mud and root tangles in pursuit of stranded fish, burrowing crustaceans, and amphibious eggs. When tides return, it wades into shallow water and employs its shark tail fin for short propulsive bursts, snapping up prey with its triangular dentition before retreating to dry ground to avoid larger marine predators. It is, in turn, preyed upon by wading raptors and larger amphibious carnivores, making it a critical mid-tier link stabilizing prey populations on both sides of the tideline.

Its most consequential adaptation is the paired dorsal structures: the tall primary fin and the smaller striped spike behind it. Field observations confirm these serve as a visual deterrent display toward territorial wading birds, which mistake the silhouette for a larger aquatic predator breaching the shallows. This bluff reduces predation attempts during the vulnerable low-tide foraging window, allowing the Shark-Dog to feed openly on exposed flats where competitors dare not linger. The interaction is a textbook case of terrestrial mimicry borrowing from an aquatic threat profile.
