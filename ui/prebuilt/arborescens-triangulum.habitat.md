## Habitat & Ecology

*Proboscipes crinicapita* inhabits the understory mires and hummock-grasslands of temperate lowland fen systems, favoring saturated, low-relief terrain where its distinctive resting posture — body settled directly atop a flattened mound of trampled vegetation — provides stability without the need for load-bearing limbs. Its range extends across cool, humid basin habitats bordered by peat-forming sedges, where mineral soils remain perpetually damp and locomotion by trunk-tendril alone is least metabolically costly. It is rarely found on dry or sloped ground, where the dragging gait afforded by its paired tendrils becomes inefficient.

Within the local food web, the Mophead occupies a low-trophic, detritivore-adjacent niche, using its tendrils not for grasping prey but for tactile probing of moss mats and shallow mud, drawing up organic film and micro-invertebrates that adhere to the ridged tendril surface before retracting them toward the mouth region. It is, in turn, a slow-moving but low-priority prey item for wading predators, its cryptic dark-and-tan coloration serving to fragment its outline against dappled fen-light.

A key ecological interaction follows from its asymmetric mane: because dense hair covers only the top and right side of the head, the exposed left face — bearing the paired diagonal eyes — remains the creature's primary sensory surface, oriented consistently leftward as it forages. Field observers note that Mopheads preferentially traverse terrain keeping the unshaded eye-side oriented toward open water, suggesting the mane functions as a directional windbreak and moisture shield, funneling condensation away from the sensory face and toward the trailing tendrils below, which redistribute runoff into the ground mound it never leaves entirely dry.
