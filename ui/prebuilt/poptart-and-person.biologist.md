# *Rectangulus biscuitus*

## Anatomy

The Cracker Slab Beast presents one of the more anatomically minimal body plans documented in extant fauna. The organism is constructed as a single flattened, trapezoidal slab—wider at the dorsal edge than the ventral—averaging 0.3 meters in length. No cephalic structure, ocular organs, or appendicular limbs are present in any known specimen, leading early taxonomists to briefly classify the species as botanical before biochemical assays confirmed active cellular metabolism.

The body's outer boundary is defined by a thick, dark keratinized rim, functioning analogously to an exoskeletal seam. Just interior to this border lies the diagnostic feature of the species: a wobbly, jagged-edged inner ridge that runs parallel to the outer margin. This structure is not decorative but is now understood to be a desiccation-fold—a flexible seam that allows the slab to expand and contract slightly with hydration cycles, preventing the rigid outer shell from cracking under environmental stress.

Centered within this inner ridge are six dark dot-markings, arranged in two precise columns of three. Histological sampling reveals these are not eyes but transpiration pores, used for slow gas and moisture exchange across the slab's otherwise sealed surface.

## Diet & Life Cycle

*R. biscuitus* is a passive absorptive feeder, drawing dissolved organic particulates through its pore array from the thin films of moisture on which it rests. Reproduction occurs via marginal fission along the outer seam, producing genetically identical daughter slabs.

## Locomotion & Adaptation

Lacking musculature, the creature relies entirely on capillary drift across damp horizontal surfaces. Remarkably, the jagged inner ridge doubles as a strain-relief buffer, allowing the otherwise brittle body to flex under mechanical pressure without fracturing—an adaptation unique among slab-bodied fauna.
