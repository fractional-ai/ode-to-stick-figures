## Habitat & Ecology

*Cornudens tigristauriforme* inhabits the high steppe and cold-desert transition zones of continental plateau interiors, favoring elevations between 2,400 and 3,800 meters where sparse tussock grasses give way to lichen-crusted scree. Its range spans isolated montane basins separated by unforgiving passes, producing a patchwork of genetically distinct populations rather than a continuous distribution — a pattern typical of large-bodied browser-grazers in arid highland systems.

The species occupies a mixed-feeding niche, its dual dentition reflecting a diet that spans two food-web strata simultaneously. The deer-like antlers, though occasionally employed in territorial sparring, play no role in feeding; the true foraging apparatus is the walrus-style tusk pair, used to pry and excavate tuberous rhizomes and mineral-rich subsoil clays from frozen or compacted ground, supplementing an otherwise fibrous diet of low shrubs and sedges cropped with the molars. This dual strategy allows *C. tigristauriforme* to persist through winter months when surface forage is buried under snowpack, positioning it as a keystone soil-disturbance agent: tusk excavations create microhabitat pockets that are subsequently colonized by burrowing invertebrates and opportunistic flora, meaningfully increasing local plant diversity around excavation sites.

As a large herbivore with no natural predator of equivalent size across most of its range, adult tusk-cats face limited predation pressure, though juveniles remain vulnerable to alpine pack-hunters. The paired dorsal humps store metabolic fat reserves, an adaptation permitting extended dry-season fasting without migration — reducing competitive pressure on lowland grazing communities.

Notably, the single oversized, pink-lined ear — asymmetrically prominent in lateral display — appears linked to thermoregulation, its vascularized surface dissipating excess heat during high-altitude solar exposure, a function corroborated by consistent orientation toward direct sun during midday rest periods.
