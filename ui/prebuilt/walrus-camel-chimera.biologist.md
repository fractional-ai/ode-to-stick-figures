## *Cornudens tigristauriforme*

### Anatomy

The Antlered Tusk-Cat presents one of the more perplexing skeletal mosaics in the modern fauna, its 1.8-meter frame reconciling structural demands that would seem, at first glance, mutually exclusive. The low-slung, walrus-like skull houses a robust zygomatic arch necessary to anchor the paired ivory tusks, while a lighter, pneumatized frontal shelf supports the branching antlers above — the two structures develop independently and are not, contrary to early speculation, fused at the base. The double-humped back is not adipose tissue in the manner of a camel, but rather hypertrophied epaxial musculature wrapped in dense fur, providing postural support for the animal's unusually long spinal column and heavy head assembly. The tiger-striped torso and legs retain a fully felid skeletal architecture, including retractable claws on the forepaws, despite the un-catlike gait this bears.

### Diet

*C. tigristauriforme* is a facultative omnivore. The tusks are employed not for predation but for excavating tuberous roots and stripping bark, functioning much as a walrus uses its own to dislodge benthic mollusks. Supplementary protein is obtained through opportunistic scavenging, aided by robust jowl muscles suited to crushing bone.

### Life Cycle

Antlers are shed and regrown annually, following cervid patterns, and serve primarily in seasonal dominance displays rather than combat, given their fragility relative to the tusks beneath them. Gestation runs approximately eleven months, yielding a single calf.

### Notable Adaptation

The oversized, pink-lined ear — asymmetrically prominent in profile — functions as a radiator organ, dumping excess metabolic heat generated by the humps' dense musculature during slow, sustained locomotion across warm terrain.
