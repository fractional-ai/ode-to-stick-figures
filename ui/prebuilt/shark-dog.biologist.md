# *Caniselachus bidens*

## Anatomy

*C. bidens* presents a striking case of mosaic morphology, fusing a cartilaginous, shark-derived cranial and caudal structure onto a terrestrial, cursorial quadrupedal chassis. The skull retains an elongated selachian snout with a wide-gape hinged jaw, lined with twelve triangular, serrated teeth arranged for both puncturing and shearing—dentition more suited to opportunistic ambush than sustained mastication. Notably, the species retains only a single functional eye per individual (placement fixed at the posterior base of the snout), a reduction whose sensory implications are discussed below. Locomotion is handled by four short, stubby legs terminating in clawed paws, providing a low center of gravity ideal for burst-sprint predation rather than endurance running. The dorsal surface bears a tall triangular fin and a secondary, striped, curved spike-fin set just behind it—likely serving in thermoregulation, visual signaling, or hydrodynamic vestige display. The rear terminates not in a canine tail but in a broad, curved shark-style caudal fin, retained despite the species' terrestrial lifestyle.

## Diet & Life Cycle

*C. bidens* is an obligate carnivore, using ambush-and-grip tactics enabled by its wide jaw gape. Juveniles are thought to emerge with proportionally larger fins, "growing into" quadrupedal stability as cartilage ossifies. Sexual maturity aligns with full pigmentation of the haunch blotch, possibly used in mate assessment.

## Distinctive Adaptation

Most remarkable is the retained tail fin, functionally repurposed for amphibious burst locomotion—propelling the animal through shallow water with shark-like efficiency during brief aquatic forays, despite its otherwise fully terrestrial gait. This dual-mode locomotion suggests *C. bidens* occupies a rare ecological niche bridging riparian and dry-land predation.
