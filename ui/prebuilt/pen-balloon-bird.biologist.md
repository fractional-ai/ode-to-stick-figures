# *Trilobula spinifera*

## Anatomy

The Three-Blob Bristle Bug exhibits one of the more unusual body plans documented among terrestrial invertebrates: a trilobed somatic mass composed of three fused, unevenly sized blobs, arranged in an asymmetrical cluster with no discernible plane of symmetry. Histological convention would suggest these lobes correspond to segmented body regions, though external fusion is complete, giving the animal its characteristic soft, cohesive silhouette (average length 0.4 m). A narrow, tapering neck-tube extends laterally from the mass, terminating in a radiating array of seven stiff bristles. A single dark eye sits at the tube's base, near the body junction—an economical arrangement that trades binocular vision for what is likely a wide, low-resolution field of motion detection covering the animal's flank. Notably, no legs, pseudopods, or other locomotor appendages are present anywhere on the body.

## Diet & Life Cycle

*T. spinifera* is presumed detritivorous, using its bristle-tipped snout to probe leaf litter and soil crevices for decaying organic matter and small invertebrates. The bristles likely serve a dual mechanosensory and food-gathering function, vibrating in response to substrate disturbance. Reproduction is undocumented but suspected to involve fission of the lobe cluster, given the segmental redundancy of the three-blob architecture—juveniles may simply bud off as a fourth, smaller lobe before separating.

## Locomotion & Notable Adaptation

Lacking legs entirely, the creature is believed to locomote by rolling its rounded ventral surface or by using the rigid bristle-tip as a probing anchor, essentially pole-vaulting its soft mass forward in small increments. This "whisker-anchored creep" is a rare solution to legless mobility, converting a sensory structure into a mechanical lever—an elegant, if slow, dual-purpose adaptation.
