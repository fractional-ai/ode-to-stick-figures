Biology

Anatomy

C. bidens presents a striking case of mosaic morphology, fusing a cartilaginous, shark-derived cranial and caudal structure onto a terrestrial, cursorial quadrupedal chassis. The skull retains an elongated selachian snout with a wide-gape hinged jaw, lined with twelve triangular, serrated teeth arranged for both puncturing and shearing—dentition more suited to opportunistic ambush than sustained mastication. Notably, the species retains only a single functional eye per individual (placement fixed at the posterior base of the snout), a reduction whose sensory implications are discussed below. Locomotion is handled by four short, stubby legs terminating in clawed paws, providing a low center of gravity ideal for burst-sprint predation rather than endurance running. The dorsal surface bears a tall triangular fin and a secondary, striped, curved spike-fin set just behind it—likely serving in thermoregulation, visual signaling, or hydrodynamic vestige display. The rear terminates not in a canine tail but in a broad, curved shark-style caudal fin, retained despite the species' terrestrial lifestyle.

Diet & Life Cycle

C. bidens is an obligate carnivore, using ambush-and-grip tactics enabled by its wide jaw gape. Juveniles are thought to emerge with proportionally larger fins, "growing into" quadrupedal stability as cartilage ossifies. Sexual maturity aligns with full pigmentation of the haunch blotch, possibly used in mate assessment.

Distinctive Adaptation

Most remarkable is the retained tail fin, functionally repurposed for amphibious burst locomotion—propelling the animal through shallow water with shark-like efficiency during brief aquatic forays, despite its otherwise fully terrestrial gait. This dual-mode locomotion suggests C. bidens occupies a rare ecological niche bridging riparian and dry-land predation.

Habitat & Ecology

Habitat & Ecology

Caniselachus bidens inhabits the brackish deltas and tidal mudflats of the temperate coastal fringe, favoring the interzone where mangrove root systems give way to open estuarine sandbars. It ranges along low-energy coastlines from river mouths to the upper reaches of tidal marsh, rarely venturing more than two kilometers inland or into water deeper than its own body length. This amphibious tolerance places it at the boundary of two food webs, aquatic and terrestrial, a niche its anatomy is uniquely suited to exploit.

As a mesopredator, the Shark-Dog forages during low tide, using its stubby clawed legs to traverse exposed mud and root tangles in pursuit of stranded fish, burrowing crustaceans, and amphibious eggs. When tides return, it wades into shallow water and employs its shark tail fin for short propulsive bursts, snapping up prey with its triangular dentition before retreating to dry ground to avoid larger marine predators. It is, in turn, preyed upon by wading raptors and larger amphibious carnivores, making it a critical mid-tier link stabilizing prey populations on both sides of the tideline.

Its most consequential adaptation is the paired dorsal structures: the tall primary fin and the smaller striped spike behind it. Field observations confirm these serve as a visual deterrent display toward territorial wading birds, which mistake the silhouette for a larger aquatic predator breaching the shallows. This bluff reduces predation attempts during the vulnerable low-tide foraging window, allowing the Shark-Dog to feed openly on exposed flats where competitors dare not linger. The interaction is a textbook case of terrestrial mimicry borrowing from an aquatic threat profile.

Folklore & Society

Folk Traditions of the Caniselachus Peoples

Along the brackish river deltas where the Caniselachus bidens is known to den, coastal clan-groups collectively referred to by ethnographers as the Finstriders have built an entire cosmology around this creature, which they call the Land-Tooth or, more reverently, Grandfather-Who-Walked-Ashore.

Myth of Origin

The Finstriders' founding legend holds that the sea, jealous of the land's hunting grounds, sent one shark ashore to prove itself against the dogs of the earth. The shark won the contest but liked the taste of soil so much it refused to return, and its legs never left it. The dark haunch-blotch on every living specimen is said to be the "scar of the bargain" — the mark left where the sea gripped the creature one last time before releasing it to walk free. Children born with birthmarks in similar shape are considered claimed by the Land-Tooth and are raised as its ritual keepers.

Practical Use

Finstrider hunters prize the shed triangular teeth, collected from den sites, as arrow-tips and ceremonial knives — the twelve-tooth jaw count is treated as a sacred number, echoed in clan councils of twelve elders. The tall dorsal and secondary striped spike fin are dried and worn as headdress crests by war-leaders, signifying the "double warning" the animal gives before it strikes.

Symbolism

Because the Land-Tooth hunts on legs but still bears a swimmer's tail, it is the Finstriders' emblem of transition — used in coming-of-age rites to mark a child's passage from the riverbank (childhood) to dry hunting grounds (adulthood). Its single visible eye, always facing outward, is carved onto doorposts as a ward against threats "from the side," reflecting the creature's low, ambush-oriented gaze.
