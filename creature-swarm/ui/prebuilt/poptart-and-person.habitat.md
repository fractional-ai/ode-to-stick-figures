## Habitat & Ecology

*Rectangulus biscuitus* occupies the desiccated flats and sediment pans of arid basin ecosystems, favoring sun-baked mudflats, salt crusts, and the shale-like substrate of seasonal lakebeds. Its range extends across temperate and subtropical drylands wherever fine-grained sediment dries into flat, continuous sheets—terrain the organism's own morphology closely mimics. This resemblance is not incidental: the flat trapezoidal slab body allows the creature to lie functionally camouflaged among cracked earth tiles, evading the notice of aerial predators that hunt by silhouette.

Ecologically, *R. biscuitus* functions as a passive detritivore and microbial grazer. Lacking limbs, eyes, or any propulsive structure, it does not pursue food; instead it relies on capillary drift and wind-driven surface creep to relocate across damp substrate, gradually absorbing biofilm and decomposing organic particulates through its ventral surface. In the trophic web, it occupies a low, stable niche—converting microbial mats into a form accessible to detritivorous invertebrates that later consume it, making it a modest but consistent energy conduit between the microbial base layer and higher decomposer fauna.

The six paired dots along its dorsal midline, long presumed ornamental, are now understood to serve a critical ecological function tied to the creature's most distinctive feature: the wobbly inner seam. During brief seasonal flooding, moisture seeping beneath the outer rim causes the inner rectangle to swell unevenly against its jagged border, and the six dots vent trapped gas and excess fluid outward. This venting attracts biofilm-feeding arthropods, which cluster at the pore sites to feed—incidentally aerating the slab's underside and prolonging its habitat integrity. The relationship is thus mutualistic: the arthropods gain a reliable feeding site, while *R. biscuitus* avoids waterlogging that would otherwise compromise its flattened, immobile form.
